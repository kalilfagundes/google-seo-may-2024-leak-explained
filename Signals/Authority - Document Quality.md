# Authority — Document Quality

Google does not rank content it cannot fully read. The `Goodoc` system (251 models) and `Htmlrender` system (241 models) jointly evaluate the technical quality of how a document is structured and rendered. These are not UX guidelines — they are signals that determine whether a page is classified as a high-quality document before content signals like freshness or query coverage are applied.

`Goodoc` evaluates the logical and semantic structure of the document: heading hierarchy, content nesting, and text-to-element ratio. `Htmlrender` evaluates how well the page renders in a headless browser, including CSS validity, JavaScript execution, and final DOM state. Both feed into `CompositeDocQualitySignals`, which also stores `lastSignificantUpdate` and `pagetype` — confirming that document quality assessment happens at the same pipeline stage as freshness and content classification.

## In the Leak

Key fields from the `Goodoc` and `Htmlrender` systems:

- **`renderTreeQuality`** — a score reflecting the quality of the render tree after HTML is parsed. A malformed or deeply broken DOM directly lowers this score.
- **`renderingfailure`** — detects when rendering fails partially or fully. Pages that fail to render are treated as low-quality documents regardless of their link profile.
- **`GoodocLogicalEntity`** — represents the semantic structure of the document: headings, sections, navigation, article blocks. Correct use of HTML5 semantic tags (`<article>`, `<nav>`, `<section>`, `<header>`) feeds this field.
- **`GoodocDocumentHeaderFont`** — font analysis on headings, evaluating legibility (font size, contrast) relative to body text.
- **`CompositeDocQualitySignals`** — the parent structure containing `lastSignificantUpdate`, `pagetype`, and document-level quality scores. This is where rendering quality, freshness, and content-type signals are combined into a single document descriptor.

DOM-level signals also appear explicitly:

- **DOM size** — number of DOM nodes. Pages exceeding ~1,500 nodes are flagged as heavy.
- **Hidden/invisible elements** — `display:none` or `visibility:hidden` elements are detected. Excessive hidden content signals manipulation.
- **Text vs. non-text ratio** — pages that are mostly images, ads, or interactive elements with thin text are classified as low-quality documents.

The leak also documents a **`pageQuality` (PQ)** field that uses an LLM to estimate the **"effort"** required to produce an article. The implication is that Google is evaluating content replicability — content that could be easily generated by a tool scores lower effort, while content requiring tools, original research, images, video, or deep expertise scores higher. *(Source: [Search Engine Land](https://searchengineland.com/unpacking-googles-massive-search-documentation-leak-442716))*

The **`pagetype` classification** in `CompositeDocQualitySignals` includes specific identifiers for: news sites, YMYL, personal/small blogs, ecommerce, and video sites. This means Google applies different quality scoring pipelines depending on what category of site the page belongs to. The explicit classification of "personal blogs" as a distinct type raises questions about whether small personal sites face a different quality threshold than established publishers. *(Source: [Search Engine Land](https://searchengineland.com/unpacking-googles-massive-search-documentation-leak-442716))*

**Index storage tiers** are explicitly documented in the leak, mapping directly to document quality and update frequency: *(Source: [Search Engine Land](https://searchengineland.com/unpacking-googles-massive-search-documentation-leak-442716))*

- **Flash drives** — used for the most important and regularly updated content (primary index / Alexandria)
- **Solid state drives** — used for less important content (SegIndexer secondary tier)
- **Standard hard drives** — used for irregularly updated content (TeraGoogle long-term storage)

Content stored on standard hard drives has the lowest crawl priority and is definitionally excluded from freshness signals. An underperforming page that stops receiving traffic or links risks being demoted to HDD storage, reducing the frequency at which Google re-evaluates it.

## Confidence: High

`Goodoc` and `Htmlrender` are explicitly named systems with 492 combined documented models. `CompositeDocQualitySignals` is a named field in the leak with documented sub-fields. `renderingfailure` and `renderTreeQuality` are named scoring fields. The antitrust process confirmed that page quality signals are applied pre-ranking.

## How to Measure on Your Site

- **Google URL Inspection Tool:** Shows how Googlebot rendered your page. Rendering errors here directly correspond to `renderingfailure` signals.
- **Chrome DevTools → Lighthouse:** DOM node count, render-blocking resources, and text contrast are all covered. Keep DOM nodes under 1,500.
- **W3C HTML Validator:** Catches structural errors that lower `renderTreeQuality`.
- **PageSpeed Insights:** JavaScript execution time correlates with `Htmlrender` rendering quality.
- **Screaming Frog:** Audits H1/H2 hierarchy, missing semantic tags, and hidden content at scale.

## What to Do

**Use semantic HTML5 tags.** `<article>`, `<section>`, `<nav>`, `<header>`, `<footer>`, `<main>` feed `GoodocLogicalEntity` directly. A page structured entirely with `<div>` elements has a weaker semantic structure score than one using appropriate semantic tags.

**Maintain a clean heading hierarchy.** One `<h1>`, followed by sequential `<h2>` and `<h3>` blocks. Skipping from `<h1>` to `<h3>`, or using multiple `<h1>` tags, degrades the heading structure signal read by `Goodoc`.

**Keep the DOM lean.** Remove unused widgets, redundant wrapper divs, and hidden content that has no user function. Pages with large DOMs take longer to render and score worse on `renderTreeQuality`.

**Minimize render-blocking JavaScript.** `Htmlrender` simulates a real browser render. JavaScript that blocks the render path delays when `renderTreeQuality` is evaluated — and content only visible after JS execution may not be indexed in the initial pass.

**Avoid excessive hidden content.** Occasional `display:none` for UI interactions (accordions, tabs) is acceptable. Hiding substantial textual content from users while keeping it in the DOM is explicitly detected as invisible-element spam.

**Ensure adequate text density.** The text vs. non-text ratio check means pages heavy with images, videos, or ads with thin textual content will be classified as low-quality documents regardless of link authority.

**Test rendering on mobile.** `Htmlrender` includes mobile rendering quality. Pages that render correctly on desktop but break on mobile receive a lower document quality score.
